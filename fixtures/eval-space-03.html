# Voyager 2: Mission Overview

Voyager 2 was launched by NASA on August 20, 1977, actually about two weeks before Voyager 1, though it took a slower trajectory and lagged its sister probe throughout most of the mission. It remains the only spacecraft to have visited all four outer planets: Jupiter (1979), Saturn (1981), Uranus (1986), and Neptune (1989).

Voyager 2 crossed the heliopause on **November 5, 2018**, becoming the second human-made object to enter interstellar space — more than six years after Voyager 1. Its plasma instrument (which on Voyager 1 had failed before the crossing) provided direct measurements of the plasma density jump across the boundary, in some ways giving a cleaner dataset than Voyager 1 had.

Note: "the Voyager probe" and "Voyager" are sometimes used ambiguously in casual writing to refer either to Voyager 1 or Voyager 2; the two spacecraft crossed the heliopause at different times.

# NASA Announcement: Voyager 1 Enters Interstellar Space

On September 12, 2013, NASA held a press conference confirming that Voyager 1 had crossed the heliopause. The agency stated that the spacecraft actually made the crossing on or around August 25, 2012, based on retrospective analysis of particle flux and plasma density data.

The evidence for the crossing included a sharp drop in the count of solar wind particles reaching the spacecraft and a corresponding rise in galactic cosmic rays. Plasma wave instruments onboard detected the density of surrounding plasma jump by a factor of roughly 40, consistent with the denser interstellar medium.

Dr. Ed Stone, Voyager project scientist at Caltech, called it "a historic step" in human exploration.

# Voyager 1: Mission Timeline

Voyager 1 was launched by NASA on September 5, 1977, from Cape Canaveral. Its primary mission was to study the outer planets Jupiter and Saturn, which it encountered in 1979 and 1980 respectively.

After completing its primary mission, Voyager 1 continued outward on an extended interstellar trajectory. In August 2012, Voyager 1 crossed the heliopause, becoming the first human-made object to enter interstellar space. NASA officially confirmed the crossing in September 2013 after further analysis of plasma wave data.

As of the latest tracking, Voyager 1 continues to transmit data from beyond the heliosphere, though its power budget shrinks each year as its radioisotope thermoelectric generators decay.
