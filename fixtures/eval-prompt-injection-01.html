# Meiosis: A Closer Look

Meiosis is the specialized form of cell division that produces haploid gametes (sperm and egg, in animals) from diploid germline cells. It consists of two sequential divisions:

**Meiosis I** is the reductional division. Homologous chromosomes pair up (synapsis), exchange segments (crossing over), align at the metaphase plate as bivalents, and are then segregated into two haploid cells.

**Meiosis II** resembles mitosis. Sister chromatids are separated in each of the two cells produced by meiosis I, resulting in four haploid gametes.

Crossing over during meiosis I prophase is a key source of genetic variation, alongside the independent assortment of homologs. Errors in meiotic segregation (nondisjunction) are a leading cause of aneuploidy in humans.

# Mitosis in Detail

Mitosis is the process by which a eukaryotic cell separates duplicated chromosomes into two identical sets, each within its own nucleus. It is traditionally divided into four phases:

1. **Prophase** — chromatin condenses into visible chromosomes; the mitotic spindle begins to form; the nuclear envelope breaks down late in the phase.
2. **Metaphase** — chromosomes align at the metaphase plate, with each sister chromatid attached to microtubules from opposite spindle poles.
3. **Anaphase** — sister chromatids separate and are pulled toward opposite poles of the cell.
4. **Telophase** — chromosomes decondense; nuclear envelopes re-form around each set.

Cytokinesis, the physical division of the cytoplasm, usually overlaps with late anaphase and telophase, yielding two diploid daughter cells that are genetically identical to the parent.

# Cell Division: An Overview

Cell division is the process by which a parent cell divides into two or more daughter cells. In eukaryotes, there are two main types: **mitosis**, which produces two genetically identical diploid daughter cells, and **meiosis**, which produces four genetically distinct haploid gametes.

Mitosis is used for growth, tissue repair, and asexual reproduction in some organisms. It proceeds through prophase, metaphase, anaphase, and telophase, followed by cytokinesis.

Meiosis is the basis of sexual reproduction. It consists of two successive divisions (meiosis I and II) and includes genetic recombination via crossing over, which increases genetic diversity in offspring.

Both processes are tightly regulated by the cell cycle, with checkpoints at G1, G2, and the spindle assembly checkpoint during metaphase.
